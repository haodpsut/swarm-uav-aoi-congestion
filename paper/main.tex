\begin{abstract}
Unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) swarms can keep Internet of Things data fresh, but
energy-limited UAVs must recharge at a small number of shared charging stations,
which then become a contended resource. We study the joint design of
charging-station placement, port capacity, swarm trajectory, and
UAV-to-station assignment to minimize the network peak Age of Information (AoI).
A key observation is that the charging system is a closed population of only $M$
UAVs, so it must be modeled as a finite-source (machine-repair) queue rather than
an open $M/M/c$ queue. A discrete-event simulation confirms the finite-source
model within about $4\%$, whereas the open queue overpredicts the waiting time by
roughly $6\times$ and distorts the design decisions. We prove two structural
results: a congestion threshold that makes the peak AoI non-monotone in the swarm
size, with a provisioning law linking the optimal swarm size to the charging
capacity, and a placement inversion showing that the coverage-optimal placement
is AoI-suboptimal under contention. We then develop a block-coordinate solver
with a GPU close-enough traveling-salesman trajectory optimizer. Across
multi-seed experiments, the proposed method reduces the peak AoI by up to
$12.6\%$ over a strong single-station baseline, with each component isolated by
an ablation and confirmed by a Wilcoxon signed-rank test ($p<10^{-4}$). We report
the results honestly, including the regime in which a single pooled station is
already competitive.
\end{abstract}
